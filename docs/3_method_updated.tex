\section{The adaptive PINN method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Overview of the Method}
\label{sec:overview}

The tree based structure described in
Section~\ref{sec:background} form a natural starting point for a wide range of domain decomposition
designs that combine local models into a Mixture of Experts (MoE). In this
section we present the method adopted in this work, which we refer to as
AToE (Adaptive Tree of Experts). Section~\ref{sec:additional_methods}
describes additional variants of the main algorithm. Throughout, we
deliberately keep the training scheme as clean as possible in order to
demonstrate the advantages of the method itself. The enhancement techniques
mentioned in Section~\ref{sec:introduction} and described in more detail in
Section~\ref{sec:additional_methods} may be applied on top of it and can
further improve results, as illustrated by several examples in
Section~\ref{sec:experiments}.

\noindent The full procedure is summarized in Algorithm~\ref{alg:atoe}, Where each of
its stages is described in detail in the subsections that follow.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{AToE: Adaptive Tree of Experts}
\label{alg:atoe}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require PDE problem, architectures $\mathcal{A}_{\mathrm{base}}$, $\mathcal{A}_{\mathrm{exp}}$, budget $M$
\State Train a vanilla PINN $u_0$ with $\mathcal{A}_{\mathrm{base}}$ to convergence
\State Build a decision tree from $u_0$; trim to an $M$-term complete binary tree
\State Record $u_0$ on the leaf interfaces
\ForAll{leaf regions $\Omega_j$ \textbf{in parallel}}
    \State Train an expert $u_j$ with $\mathcal{A}_{\mathrm{exp}}$ on $\Omega_j$, with interface data from $u_0$
\EndFor
\State Blend the experts via smoothstep POU (partition of unity) weights
\State Fine tune the blended composition under the global loss
\Ensure Global approximate solution $u_\theta$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Root Model Training}
\label{sec:root_training}

The root model $u_0$ follows the classic PINN formulation described in
Section~\ref{sec:background}. For the second order benchmarks we use an MLP
with 4 hidden layers of 60 neurons. For the third and fourth order benchmarks
we use the same architecture combined with a time marching mechanism: the time
axis is split into windows (4 for KdV, 10 for KS), each window is trained in
turn, and the prediction of the converged window at its final time serves as
the initial condition for the next. Training consists of 5k Adam iterations
with learning rate $10^{-3}$, followed by 40k SSBroyden iterations. Both
stages use full batch training with 10k residual points and 256 points each
for the initial and boundary conditions, and all computations are performed in
double precision, as suggested in~\cite{kiyani2025optimizer}.

\vspace{0.5em}

\subsection{Tree Construction and Domain Decomposition}
\label{sec:tree_construction}

The goal of the decomposition is to partition the domain into regions that
reflect the local complexity of the solution. A geometric wavelet tree
$\mathcal{T}$ is constructed from the converged root approximation $u_0$ and
compressed using an $M$-term approximation, retaining the $M$ nodes with the
largest geometric wavelet norms $\|\psi_\Omega\|$. To preserve a clean
hierarchical structure, both the ancestors and the siblings of the selected
nodes are always retained, so that the accepted regions form a complete binary
tree in which every point of the domain is covered.

\noindent When time-marching is employed, the global budget $M$ is distributed
across the windows according to a prescribed allocation strategy (e.g.,
uniform, linear, or quadratic). An independent $M_i$-term tree is then
constructed within each window, with $\sum_i M_i = M$.

\noindent Each leaf region $\Omega_j \in \mathcal{L}$ of the resulting tree is
assigned a neural network expert $u_j$ with the expert architecture
$\mathcal{A}_{\mathrm{exp}}$, which is smaller than the root architecture. Ee
use an MLP with 3 hidden layers of 20 neurons. Examples of the resulting
decompositions for several benchmark PDEs are shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:mterm_trees}.

\subsection{Interpretation of the Adaptive Decomposition}
\label{sec:interpretation}

As discussed in Section~\ref{sec:background}, the equivalence between the
Tree-Besov semi-norm and the geometric wavelet norm implies that the decay of
geometric wavelet coefficients is directly related to the smoothness of the
underlying function.

\noindent A large geometric wavelet norm at a single node does not necessarily
imply that the corresponding region is non-smooth, since nodes near the root
of the tree may simply capture large scale variations of the solution.
Instead, smoothness is reflected in how rapidly the geometric wavelet norms
decay along a branch of the tree. Regions associated with branches whose
coefficients remain significant at increasing depths exhibit slower decay and
therefore correspond to lower regularity and higher local complexity.

\noindent Viewed through this lens, the tree construction procedure described
in Section~\ref{sec:tree_construction} naturally favors the retention of
branches that exhibit slow coefficient decay across multiple levels of the
hierarchy. Consequently, the resulting decomposition concentrates refinement
in the less smooth and more complex portions of the domain, while maintaining
a coarser representation in regions where the coefficient decay is more rapid.

\noindent Extended methods that take further advantage of the theory presented
here are detailed in Section~\ref{sec:additional_methods}. However, even the
simple choice of a fixed expert architecture already applies a form of
adaptive capacity allocation. Regions associated with branches that persist to
greater depths receive a larger number of experts and therefore a larger
effective approximation capacity, whereas smoother regions are represented by
shallower portions of the tree. As illustrated in
Figure~\ref{fig:capacity_maps}, this mechanism produces a nonlinear
distribution of capacity across the domain that reflects the underlying
complexity of the solution.

\subsection{Expert Training}
\label{sec:expert_training}

Rather than training the experts through the global loss terms, we exploit a
core MoE idea and let each expert solve a localized, and therefore easier,
problem on its own subdomain. Following the interface treatment
of~\cite{luo2025pdd}, each local problem is closed by taking the values on the
shared interfaces from the converged root. The predictions of $u_0$ are
recorded on the interior faces of the leaf regions and serve as boundary data
for the local problems. Concretely, for each leaf $j$ we minimize a local
objective
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_j
= \lambda_r\,\mathcal{L}^{\mathrm{res}}_j
+ \lambda_{ic}\,\mathcal{L}^{\mathrm{ic}}_j
+ \lambda_{bc}\,\mathcal{L}^{\mathrm{bc}}_j
+ \lambda_{\Gamma}\,\mathcal{L}^{\Gamma}_j,
\label{eq:leaf_local_loss}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{L}^{\mathrm{res}}_j$ enforces the PDE residual on collocation
points inside $\Omega_j$, and $\mathcal{L}^{\mathrm{ic}}_j$,
$\mathcal{L}^{\mathrm{bc}}_j$ enforce the true initial and boundary conditions
on the parts of $\partial\Omega_j$ that coincide with the physical boundary of
$\Omega$. The interface term $\mathcal{L}^{\Gamma}_j$ acts on the interior
faces $\Gamma_j$ that $\Omega_j$ shares with its neighbors. Let
$X^{\Gamma}_j$ denote the set of sample points on $\Gamma_j$. The loss ties
the local solution to the frozen root prediction $u_0$:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}^{\Gamma}_j
= \frac{1}{|X^{\Gamma}_j|}
\sum_{(x,t)\in X^{\Gamma}_j}
\bigl\| u_j(x,t) - u_0(x,t) \bigr\|^2 .
\label{eq:interface_loss}
\end{equation}
In this way every expert learns a well-posed local PDE whose boundary data on
the interior interfaces is supplied by the root, so the leaves can be trained
independently, and in parallel, while remaining mutually consistent.

\noindent When model size is not a concern, one may set
$\mathcal{A}_{\mathrm{exp}} = \mathcal{A}_{\mathrm{base}}$ and initialize each
expert by copying the weights of the root, so that at initialization every
expert reproduces the root prediction on its region. This warm start reduces
expert training time. The experts are trained with the same scheme as the root
(Section~\ref{sec:root_training}). The loss
weights $\lambda_r$, $\lambda_{ic}$, and $\lambda_{bc}$ are kept from the root
training, and the interface weight is set to $\lambda_{\Gamma} = 1$. Examples of interface training samples for inner domain experts are shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:local_expert_training}.

\subsection{Smooth Blending and Global Fine-Tuning}
\label{sec:smoothstep_windows}

The trained leaf experts are combined into a single global approximation
through smooth, compactly supported window functions. Let
$\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^d\times[0,T]$ denote the computational domain, and let
$\{\Omega_i\}_{i=0}^{k}$ be the set of regions obtained from the hierarchical
decision tree decomposition, where each region is an axis-aligned hypercube
\[
\Omega_i = [a_{i,1}, b_{i,1}] \times \cdots \times [a_{i,d+1}, b_{i,d+1}].
\]

\noindent To smoothly blend the contributions of different experts while
ensuring compatibility with the PDE's differential order, we define compactly
supported window functions with configurable smoothness. Let $N$ denote the
smoothness order, chosen such that $N$ is at least the highest order of any
partial derivative appearing in the PDE (e.g., $N \geq 2$ for Burgers or
Allen--Cahn, $N \geq 3$ for KdV, $N \geq 4$ for KS). Since the domain
decomposition operates over all dimensions including time, no dimension
receives special treatment and the window must be sufficiently smooth for all
derivatives.

\noindent We first define the smoothstep polynomial $S_N : [0,1] \to [0,1]$,
which satisfies $S_N(0) = 0$, $S_N(1) = 1$, and
$S_N^{(k)}(0) = S_N^{(k)}(1) = 0$ for $k = 1, \ldots, N$:
\begin{align}
S_1(t) &= 3t^2 - 2t^3 \quad \text{($C^1$)} \\
S_2(t) &= 6t^5 - 15t^4 + 10t^3 \quad \text{($C^2$)} \\
S_3(t) &= 35t^4 - 84t^5 + 70t^6 - 20t^7 \quad \text{($C^3$)} \\
S_4(t) &= 126t^5 - 420t^6 + 540t^7 - 315t^8 + 70t^9 \quad \text{($C^4$)}
\end{align}

\noindent The one-sided compact ramp function is defined as:
\begin{equation}
\rho_N(s) = \begin{cases}
0 & \text{if } s \leq 0 \\
S_N(s) & \text{if } 0 < s < 1 \\
1 & \text{if } s \geq 1
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

\noindent For region $\Omega_i$ with bounds $[a_{i,j}, b_{i,j}]$ along
dimension $j$, let $\delta_{i,j} = \alpha (b_{i,j} - a_{i,j})$ denote the
collar (transition) half-width, where $\alpha > 0$ is a configurable fraction
(typically $\alpha = 0.2$). The one dimensional window along dimension $j$ is:
\begin{equation}
\omega_{i,j}(x_j) = \rho_N\!\left(\frac{x_j - (a_{i,j} - \delta_{i,j})}{\delta_{i,j}}\right) \cdot \rho_N\!\left(\frac{(b_{i,j} + \delta_{i,j}) - x_j}{\delta_{i,j}}\right)
\label{eq:window_1d}
\end{equation}

\noindent This produces a flat-top window: $\omega_{i,j}(x_j) = 1$ for
$x_j \in [a_{i,j}, b_{i,j}]$, smooth $C^N$ ramps in the collars
$[a_{i,j} - \delta_{i,j}, a_{i,j}]$ and $[b_{i,j}, b_{i,j} + \delta_{i,j}]$,
and exactly zero outside
$[a_{i,j} - \delta_{i,j}, b_{i,j} + \delta_{i,j}]$.

\noindent The region indicator function is therefore the tensor product of the
one dimensional windows over all dimensions:
\begin{equation}
\Psi_{\Omega_i}(X) = \prod_{j=1}^{d} \omega_{i,j}(x_j),
\qquad X = (x_1,\ldots,x_d),
\label{eq:smoothstep_windows}
\end{equation}

\noindent Given the indicators, the global solution of AToE is the
partition of unity (POU) combination over the set of leaves $\mathcal{L}$ of
the trimmed tree:
\begin{equation}
u_\theta(x,t) =
\sum_{j \in \mathcal{L}}
\tilde{\Psi}_{\Omega_j}(x,t)\, u_j(x,t),
\label{eq:global_atoe}
\end{equation}
with the leaf normalization
\begin{equation}
\tilde{\Psi}_{\Omega_i}(X) = \frac{\Psi_{\Omega_i}(X)}{\sum_{j \in \mathcal{L}} \Psi_{\Omega_j}(X)}.
\label{eq:normalization_atoe}
\end{equation}
Once the leaf experts are spawned the root retires from the blend, it is used
only for the tree construction and the interface data, and the composition is
carried by the leaves alone. Example normalized blending weights are shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:normalization_weights}.

\noindent Finally, the full composition \eqref{eq:global_atoe} is jointly fine
tuned under the global PDE loss. This last stage reconciles the independently
trained experts in the collar zones where neighboring windows overlap,
producing a single, globally consistent solution. The fine tuning stage
applies the same training scheme as in Section~\ref{sec:root_training}, with a
single change: the Adam learning rate is decreased to $10^{-4}$.
