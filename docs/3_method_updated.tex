\section{Proposed Adaptive Domain Decomposition Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Overview of the Method}

We propose an adaptive domain decomposition framework in which the computational
domain is partitioned into subregions, each associated with a local neural network
expert. The decomposition mechanism is based on a decision tree structure, guided by
the theoretical connection between tree-based representations and smoothness
introduced in Section~\ref{sec:background}. In contrast, the global composition
of local experts follows a  POU (partition of unity) formulation inspired by existing
domain decomposition and MoE (Mixture of Experts) methods.

\vspace{0.5em}

\subsection{Tree Construction and Domain Decomposition}
\label{sec:tree_construction}

The goal of the decomposition is to partition the domain into regions that
reflect the local complexity of the solution. To better understand the desired
structure, we construct reference trees using ground truth solutions,
where the decomposition is determined directly from the geometric wavelet
representation. Examples for several benchmark PDEs are shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:perfect_trees}. These trees provide intuition regarding the
regions that are expected to receive additional refinement under an ideal
decomposition and serve as a reference for the adaptive procedures considered
in this work.

\noindent In practice, we consider two approaches for constructing the tree:

\noindent {\bf === Assaf: Should the incremental splitting be moved to the future work / discussion?}\\
\subsubsection{Incremental splitting:} training is performed for a fixed number
of epochs (or until a convergence criterion), after which a single split is
introduced by fitting a shallow decision tree (depth 1). The split is accepted
if it satisfies a norm-based criterion motivated by the theory. This approach may be advantageous when an initial vanilla PINN is unable to
capture sufficient structure of the solution to reliably estimate a complete
tree.

\subsubsection{$M$-term tree construction:} following MoE
training practices that advocate training components
separately, the root model is first
trained to convergence using a standard PINN formulation. This root model
serves a dual purpose: it provides the approximation from which the geometric
wavelet tree is constructed, and it is subsequently reused as the base expert
in the final adaptive composition. A geometric wavelet tree is then
constructed from this converged approximation and compressed using an $M$-term
approximation, retaining the $M$ largest geometric wavelet coefficients.
To preserve a clean hierarchical structure, both the ancestors and the
siblings of the selected nodes are always retained, so that the accepted
regions form a complete binary tree.
When time-window training is employed, the global budget $M$ is distributed across the windows according to a prescribed allocation strategy (e.g., uniform, linear, or quadratic). An independent $M_i$-term tree is then constructed within each window, with $\sum_i M_i = M$.

\noindent The $M$-term construction avoids the need to manually select a wavelet-norm
threshold and is generally more robust to such choices. Furthermore, it
reduces the interaction between training and tree construction, providing a
stable initialization of the decomposition while preserving the most
significant structures identified by the geometric wavelet representation. Examples of the
resulting decompositions for several benchmark PDEs are shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:mterm_trees}.

\subsection{Local Neural Network Experts}

Each subdomain is associated with a neural network expert. We consider standard
architectures such as multilayer perceptrons and residual networks.

\noindent The capacity assigned to each expert may be adapted according to the
local complexity of the corresponding region. In particular, regions associated
with larger geometric wavelet norms may require larger models, while smoother
regions may be represented using smaller networks. This possibility is motivated
by the relationship between geometric wavelet coefficients, smoothness, and
approximation rates established in the previous section.

\vspace{0.5em}

\subsection{Compact Smoothstep Windows}
\label{sec:smoothstep_windows}

Let $\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^d$ denote the computational domain, and let
$\{\Omega_i\}_{i=0}^{k}$ be the set of regions obtained from the hierarchical
decision tree decomposition, where each region is an axis-aligned hypercube
\[
\Omega_i = [a_{i,1}, b_{i,1}] \times \cdots \times [a_{i,d}, b_{i,d}].
\]
Each region $\Omega_i$ is associated with a neural network expert $u_i(x,t)$.

\noindent To smoothly blend the contributions of different experts while ensuring
compatibility with the PDE's differential order, we define compactly supported
window functions with configurable smoothness. Let $N$ denote the smoothness
order, chosen such that $N$ is at least the highest order of any partial
derivative appearing in the PDE (e.g., $N \geq 2$ for Burgers or Allen--Cahn,
$N \geq 3$ for KdV, $N \geq 4$ for KS). Since the domain decomposition
operates over all dimensions including time, no dimension receives special
treatment and the window must be sufficiently smooth for all derivatives.

\noindent We first define the smoothstep polynomial $S_N : [0,1] \to [0,1]$, which
satisfies $S_N(0) = 0$, $S_N(1) = 1$, and $S_N^{(k)}(0) = S_N^{(k)}(1) = 0$
for $k = 1, \ldots, N$:
\begin{align}
S_1(t) &= 3t^2 - 2t^3 \quad \text{($C^1$)} \\
S_2(t) &= 6t^5 - 15t^4 + 10t^3 \quad \text{($C^2$)} \\
S_3(t) &= 35t^4 - 84t^5 + 70t^6 - 20t^7 \quad \text{($C^3$)} \\
S_4(t) &= 126t^5 - 420t^6 + 540t^7 - 315t^8 + 70t^9 \quad \text{($C^4$)}
\end{align}

\noindent The one-sided compact ramp function is defined as:
\begin{equation}
\rho_N(s) = \begin{cases}
0 & \text{if } s \leq 0 \\
S_N(s) & \text{if } 0 < s < 1 \\
1 & \text{if } s \geq 1
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

\noindent For region $\Omega_i$ with bounds $[a_{i,j}, b_{i,j}]$ along dimension $j$,
let $\delta_{i,j} = \alpha (b_{i,j} - a_{i,j})$ denote the collar (transition)
half-width, where $\alpha > 0$ is a configurable fraction (typically $\alpha = 0.2$).
The one-dimensional window along dimension $j$ is:
\begin{equation}
\omega_{i,j}(x_j) = \rho_N\!\left(\frac{x_j - (a_{i,j} - \delta_{i,j})}{\delta_{i,j}}\right) \cdot \rho_N\!\left(\frac{(b_{i,j} + \delta_{i,j}) - x_j}{\delta_{i,j}}\right)
\label{eq:window_1d}
\end{equation}

\noindent This produces a \emph{flat-top} window: $\omega_{i,j}(x_j) = 1$ for $x_j \in [a_{i,j}, b_{i,j}]$,
smooth $C^N$ ramps in the collars $[a_{i,j} - \delta_{i,j}, a_{i,j}]$ and
$[b_{i,j}, b_{i,j} + \delta_{i,j}]$, and \emph{exactly zero} outside
$[a_{i,j} - \delta_{i,j}, b_{i,j} + \delta_{i,j}]$.

\noindent The region indicator function is the tensor product over all dimensions:
\begin{equation}
\Psi_{\Omega_i}(X) = \prod_{j=1}^{d} \omega_{i,j}(x_j)
\label{eq:smoothstep_windows}
\end{equation}

\noindent The expert associated with the root node (corresponding to the entire domain)
has a constant indicator $\Psi_{\Omega_0}(X) = 1$ everywhere.

\subsection{Global Composition and Expert Training}
\label{sec:variants}

The hierarchical decomposition and its associated region indicator functions
$\Psi_{\Omega_i}$ define a structured collection of local experts, but they do not
by themselves prescribe how the experts should be combined into a single global
approximation, nor how they should be trained. Many creative possibilities arise
once such a structure is available, and the MoE literature offers
useful guidance on how to
exploit it. Below we
outline a spectrum of composition strategies of increasing sophistication and then
describe, in detail, the non-additive leaf formulation adopted for the experiments
reported in this paper. Throughout, the tree closure always retains both the
ancestors and the siblings of the selected nodes, so the accepted regions form a
complete binary tree in which every point of the domain is covered.\\

\subsubsection{Additive composition - AToE (Adaptive Tree of Experts).}
The most direct strategy treats each expert as an additive residual correction on
top of the converged root. The root expert contributes with coefficient
exactly one, and every other expert adds a weighted correction:
\begin{equation}
u_\theta(x,t) = u_0(x,t) + \sum_{\ell=1}^{L} \sum_{i:\,\text{level}(i)=\ell}
w_i(x,t)\, u_i(x,t)
\label{eq:global_atoe}
\end{equation}
where the per-level weights form a partition of unity over the experts at that
level,
\begin{equation}
w_i(X) = \frac{\Psi_{\Omega_i}(X)}{\sum_{k:\,\text{level}(k)=\ell(i)} \Psi_{\Omega_k}(X)}.
\label{eq:normalization_atoe}
\end{equation}
In the additive setting one may alternatively choose not to retain the siblings
and adopt a different, base-aware POU that folds the root into the
normalization, so that any region left without a dedicated expert is carried by
the base.
Because the correction is additive and organized by level, this variant naturally
admits a coarse-to-fine schedule in which one level of experts is trained at a
time with the previous levels frozen, stopping the refinement once the target
accuracy is reached; alternatively, one may normalize the indicators over the
whole tree and train all experts jointly in a single stage. Such composition and
training choices are exactly the kind explored in the MoE literature.
\\

\subsubsection{Hierarchical activation propagation - ANT (Adaptive Neural Tree).}
A more expressive strategy lets information flow along the tree. Instead of the
raw coordinates, each expert receives the last hidden layer activation
$A_{\mathrm{parent}(i)}$ of its parent and returns both a prediction and an
activation for its own children:
\begin{equation}
u_i\!\left(A_{\mathrm{parent}(i)}(x,t)\right)
= \left( u_i^{*}(x,t),\; A_i(x,t) \right).
\label{eq:ant_activation}
\end{equation}
The global solution is the POU combination over the leaves
$\mathcal{L}$,
\begin{equation}
u_\theta(x,t) = \sum_{j \in \mathcal{L}}
\tilde{\Psi}_{\Omega_j}(x,t)\, u_j^{*}(x,t),
\label{eq:global_ant}
\end{equation}
with the leaf normalization
\begin{equation}
\tilde{\Psi}_{\Omega_i}(X) = \frac{\Psi_{\Omega_i}(X)}{\sum_{j \in \mathcal{L}} \Psi_{\Omega_j}(X)}.
\label{eq:normalization_ant}
\end{equation}
By propagating activations down the tree, deeper regions are effectively
represented by deeper, composed networks. The non-smooth, complex regions that
attract additional refinement are therefore modeled by more expressive functions,
while smoother regions remain shallow. This ties model capacity to local
complexity. As in the additive case, one may also train the tree level by level:
at each stage the current leaves act as the outputs, the levels that have finished
training are frozen, and the next level of experts is grown on top of them.

\subsubsection{Non-additive leaf composition (AToE-Leaves).}
The formulation we adopt in this paper is non-additive and uses only the leaves of
the tree. Once the leaf experts are spawned the root retires from the blend, and
the global solution is the POU combination of the leaves alone:
\begin{equation}
u_\theta(x,t) =
\sum_{j \in \mathcal{L}}
\tilde{\Psi}_{\Omega_j}(x,t)\, u_j(x,t),
\label{eq:global_atoe_leaves}
\end{equation}
with
\begin{equation}
\tilde{\Psi}_{\Omega_i}(X) = \frac{\Psi_{\Omega_i}(X)}{\sum_{j \in \mathcal{L}} \Psi_{\Omega_j}(X)},
\label{eq:normalization_leaves}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{L}$ is the complete set of leaves.

\noindent Rather than training the experts through the global loss terms, we
exploit a core MoE idea and let each expert solve a localized, and therefore
easier, problem on its own subdomain. Following the interface treatment
of~\cite{luo2025pdd}, each local problem is closed by taking the values on the shared
interfaces from the converged root. Concretely, for each leaf $j$ we minimize a
local objective
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_j
= \lambda_r\,\mathcal{L}^{\mathrm{res}}_j
+ \lambda_{ic}\,\mathcal{L}^{\mathrm{ic}}_j
+ \lambda_{bc}\,\mathcal{L}^{\mathrm{bc}}_j
+ \lambda_{\Gamma}\,\mathcal{L}^{\Gamma}_j,
\label{eq:leaf_local_loss}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{L}^{\mathrm{res}}_j$ enforces the PDE residual on collocation
points inside $\Omega_j$, and $\mathcal{L}^{\mathrm{ic}}_j$,
$\mathcal{L}^{\mathrm{bc}}_j$ enforce the true initial and boundary conditions on
the parts of $\partial\Omega_j$ that coincide with the physical boundary of
$\Omega$. The interface term $\mathcal{L}^{\Gamma}_j$ acts on the interior faces
$\Gamma_j$ that $\Omega_j$ shares with its neighbors and ties the local solution to
the frozen root prediction $u_0$:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}^{\Gamma}_j
= \frac{1}{|X^{\Gamma}_j|}
\sum_{(x,t)\in X^{\Gamma}_j}
\bigl\| u_j(x,t) - u_0(x,t) \bigr\|^2 .
\label{eq:interface_loss}
\end{equation}
In this way every expert learns a well-posed local PDE whose boundary data on the
interior interfaces is supplied by the root, so the leaves can be trained
independently while remaining mutually consistent.

\noindent Finally, the trained leaves are assembled through the POU blend
\eqref{eq:global_atoe_leaves} and the full composition is jointly fine tuned under
the global PDE loss. This last stage reconciles the independently trained
experts in the collar zones where neighboring windows overlap, producing a single,
globally consistent solution.

\subsection{Training and Implementation Details}

The training procedure follows standard PINN optimization, with several enhancements
to improve convergence and stability. In particular, we incorporate:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Adaptive sampling strategies~\cite{wu2023rad}.
    \item Causal training for time-dependent problems~\cite{wang2022causal}.
    \item Adaptive loss weighting (e.g., LRA)~\cite{wang2021lra}.
    \item Fourier feature embeddings~\cite{tancik2020fourier}.
    \item Weight factorization techniques~\cite{wang2021weightfactorization}.
    \item Second-stage optimizers (e.g., L-BFGS, SSBroyden)~\cite{kiyani2025optimizer}.
    \item Double precision arithmetic.
    \item Expert initialization techniques: parent weight copying for hidden
    layers with variant specific output layer handling (zeroed for AToE additive
    residuals, copied for AToE-Leaves/ANT POU continuity).
\end{itemize}
